# Demand Generation

## Domain Overview

B2B demand generation is the integrated system of marketing programs designed to create awareness, build pipeline, and drive revenue across the full buyer journey. It encompasses both demand creation (generating new interest in a category or solution among accounts not yet in-market) and demand capture (converting existing intent into qualified pipeline). Per 6sense's 2025 B2B Buyer Experience Report, buyers complete roughly 70% of their journey before contacting a vendor, and the pre-contact favorite wins approximately 80% of the time. This fundamentally shifts demand generation from a lead-capture exercise to an influence system operating during the invisible majority of the buying cycle.

The modern demand generation function sits at the intersection of marketing, sales development, and revenue operations. The Forrester B2B Revenue Waterfall (evolved from the SiriusDecisions Demand Waterfall) provides the canonical framework: prospects flow from Anonymous → Known → Engaged → Prioritized Account → Qualified Buying Group → Pipeline Opportunity → Closed-Won. Practitioners must design programs across this entire continuum rather than optimizing for any single stage. Average B2B sales cycles run 10.1 months (6sense, 2025), with buying committees averaging 10-11 stakeholders, making multi-threaded engagement across personas mandatory rather than optional.

The discipline bifurcates into inbound programs (content marketing, SEO, webinars, organic social, community) that attract accounts showing early research behavior, and outbound programs (targeted email sequences, LinkedIn outreach, paid media, events, content syndication) that proactively engage accounts matching the Ideal Customer Profile. High-performing teams run both motions simultaneously: inbound to influence the 95% of the market not yet in buying mode, and outbound to capture the ~5% showing active intent signals. U.S. B2B digital ad spending reached $18.3 billion in 2024, growing approximately 15% annually, reflecting the scale of investment in these programs.

Lead nurturing bridges the gap between initial engagement and sales-readiness. Research consistently shows that 50% of leads are qualified but not ready to buy at the time of first interaction. Nurture programs — segmented email workflows, retargeting, personalized content tracks — maintain engagement and accelerate progression through buying stages. The MQL-to-SQL conversion rate benchmarks between 13-22% across B2B broadly, with high-performing B2B SaaS teams reaching 25-40% through advanced behavioral scoring and ICP alignment. Organizations integrating intent data with predictive scoring report 30-40% improvement in conversion rates and 40% shorter sales cycles.

## Core Decision Framework

### The Three Foundational Decisions

**1. ICP-First Program Design vs. Volume-First Program Design**
Every demand generation strategy begins with a binary architectural choice. ICP-first design starts with deep analysis of best-fit accounts (80/20 analysis on existing customer base), maps the buying committee structure, identifies where these accounts consume information, and builds programs to reach them specifically. Volume-first design casts a wide net and relies on scoring to filter. ICP-first consistently outperforms on pipeline efficiency metrics (cost-per-opportunity, win rate, deal size) but requires rigorous upfront research including customer interviews, firmographic/technographic data analysis, and jobs-to-be-done mapping.

**2. Demand Creation vs. Demand Capture Budget Allocation**
Demand creation programs (ungated thought leadership, brand podcasts, community building, original research) influence buyers during the 70% of their journey spent in anonymous research. Demand capture programs (paid search, review site optimization, demo request pages, sales outreach to intent-signaling accounts) convert buyers already in active evaluation. The practitioner's decision: what ratio of budget allocation matches the company's maturity, market position, and sales cycle length? Early-stage companies with low brand awareness need heavier demand creation (60-70% of budget). Established players in competitive markets can shift toward demand capture (50-60%) because their brand already occupies mental shortlists.

**3. Lead-Based vs. Account-Based Motion**
Traditional lead-based demand generation scores and routes individual contacts. Account-based demand generation (ABM) targets buying groups within prioritized accounts. Per industry data, 57% of B2B marketers are executing ABM programs, with 52% reporting positive ROI. The decision depends on average deal size, buying committee complexity, and total addressable market (TAM) size. Deals above $50K ACV with 6+ stakeholder buying committees almost always warrant account-based orchestration. Below $15K ACV with individual decision-makers, lead-based motions remain more cost-efficient.

### The Practitioner's Mental Model

Experienced demand generation leaders evaluate every program through the **"Pipeline Per Dollar" lens** — not cost-per-lead, not MQL volume, but the amount of qualified pipeline generated per marketing dollar invested. This metric naturally corrects for the perverse incentives that plague demand generation: it penalizes cheap but unqualified leads, rewards programs that produce high-intent engagement, and aligns marketing measurement directly with revenue outcomes. The secondary lens is **pipeline velocity** (Qualified Opportunities × Average Deal Value × Win Rate ÷ Sales Cycle Length), which forces evaluation of not just pipeline creation but pipeline acceleration.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

1. **Run 80/20 analysis on existing customers** — Identify the 20% of customers generating 80% of revenue. Document common firmographic attributes (industry, employee count, revenue range, tech stack), behavioral patterns (how they found you, what content they consumed), and buying committee composition.
2. **Conduct 8-12 customer interviews** — Focus on: what triggered their search, who was involved in the decision, what information sources they trusted, what almost disqualified you, and what ultimately tipped the decision.
3. **Define ICP and tiered account lists** — Tier 1 (exact ICP match, 100-500 accounts for ABM), Tier 2 (strong fit, 500-2,000 accounts for targeted programs), Tier 3 (addressable market for broad programs).
4. **Map the buying committee per ICP** — Document each persona: title variations, responsibilities, pain points, information consumption habits, and role in the buying decision (champion, economic buyer, technical evaluator, end user, blocker).
5. **Build the Dream 100 influence map** — Identify the 100 media properties, communities, podcasts, events, influencers, and publications where your ICP accounts consume information.

### Phase 2: Infrastructure (Weeks 3-6)

6. **Define lead lifecycle stages** — Align marketing and sales on precise stage definitions: Anonymous Visitor → Known Contact → Engaged Lead → MQL → SAL (Sales Accepted Lead) → SQL → Opportunity → Closed-Won/Lost. Document entry criteria, exit criteria, and SLAs for each stage.
7. **Build the lead scoring model** — Combine three scoring dimensions: **Fit score** (firmographic/technographic match to ICP), **Intent score** (third-party buying signals from tools like Bombora, G2, or 6sense), and **Engagement score** (behavioral activity on owned properties). Weight fit most heavily — high engagement from poor-fit accounts drains sales capacity.
8. **Configure nurture architecture** — Design segmented email workflows keyed to: (a) buying stage, (b) persona/role, and (c) pain point cluster. Build a minimum of 3 distinct nurture tracks with 6-8 touches each, spaced over 4-8 weeks. Include multi-format content (text email, video, case study, third-party validation).
9. **Establish measurement infrastructure** — Configure UTM taxonomy, CRM campaign tracking, multi-touch attribution model (W-shaped recommended: 30% first touch, 30% lead creation, 30% opportunity creation, 10% distributed), and dashboard reporting cadence.

### Phase 3: Inbound Program Launch (Weeks 5-10)

10. **Deploy content engine aligned to buying stages** — Awareness stage: original research reports, thought leadership, podcast. Consideration stage: comparison guides, webinars, solution-specific content. Decision stage: case studies, ROI calculators, implementation guides. Map each asset to a specific persona and buying stage.
11. **Launch SEO and organic strategy** — Target bottom-of-funnel keywords (solution comparisons, pricing, alternatives) for immediate pipeline impact. Build topical authority clusters around core pain points for mid-term traffic growth.
12. **Activate organic social distribution** — Prioritize LinkedIn for B2B. Develop employee advocacy program (executive + subject matter expert posting cadence). Distribute content natively rather than just linking.
13. **Build conversion infrastructure** — High-intent pages (demo request, contact sales, pricing) must convert frictionlessly. Low-intent pages should offer ungated value with optional progressive profiling.

### Phase 4: Outbound Program Launch (Weeks 6-12)

14. **Deploy targeted outbound sequences** — Build multi-channel sequences (email + LinkedIn + phone) for Tier 1 and Tier 2 accounts. Personalize the first touch with account-specific insight. Sequence length: 8-12 touches over 3-4 weeks.
15. **Launch paid media programs** — LinkedIn Ads for precise targeting by title/company/industry. Google Ads for high-intent search capture. Retargeting on both platforms for known engagers. Content syndication with lead-level targeting for Tier 2/3 accounts.
16. **Execute event and webinar strategy** — Host quarterly owned webinars addressing top ICP pain points. Sponsor 2-3 industry events per quarter with pre/post-event outbound sequences to target attendees.

### Phase 5: Optimization (Ongoing)

17. **Weekly: Monitor leading indicators** — New engaged accounts, content consumption velocity, email response rates, website traffic from ICP accounts.
18. **Bi-weekly: Review MQL-to-SQL conversion and sales feedback** — Categorize rejected MQLs by reason code. Adjust scoring model based on patterns.
19. **Monthly: Full-funnel conversion analysis** — Calculate stage-to-stage conversion rates and compare against benchmarks. Identify the single biggest drop-off point and focus optimization there.
20. **Quarterly: Program-level ROI review** — Calculate pipeline-per-dollar and customer acquisition cost by channel and program. Kill underperforming programs ruthlessly. Double down on top 2-3 performers.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Funnel Health Scorecard

| Metric | Below Average | Average | Top Performer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lead → MQL conversion | <20% | 34-41% | >50% |
| MQL → SQL conversion | <10% | 13-22% | 25-40% |
| SQL → Opportunity conversion | <25% | 36-42% | >50% |
| Opportunity → Closed-Won | <15% | 20-30% | >35% |
| Average sales cycle length | >12 months | 8-10 months | <6 months |
| Pipeline velocity (daily) | <$5K | $10-25K | >$50K |
| Marketing-sourced pipeline % | <20% | 30-40% | >50% |
| CAC payback period | >18 months | 12-15 months | <10 months |

### Program Scoring Model (Score Each 1-5)

- **ICP alignment**: Does program specifically reach defined Tier 1/2 accounts and personas?
- **Stage appropriateness**: Does content/offer match the buying stage of target audience?
- **Multi-threading potential**: Does program engage multiple buying committee members?
- **Measurability**: Can pipeline attribution be tracked to this program?
- **Scalability**: Can program volume increase without proportional cost increase?
- **Sales alignment**: Does sales team actively value and follow up on outputs?
- **Velocity impact**: Does program accelerate existing pipeline, not just create new?

## Red Flags & Edge Cases

1. **MQL volume increasing but SQL conversion declining** — Classic symptom of scoring model decay. Often caused by marketing optimizing for lead volume in quarterly reporting while actual lead quality degrades. The scoring model hasn't been recalibrated against closed-won/closed-lost analysis in 6+ months.

2. **Sales team ignoring 40%+ of MQLs without follow-up** — Indicates a broken SLA or misaligned MQL definition. When this occurs, marketing is spending budget generating leads that sales has already determined are low-quality, but the feedback loop never reaches marketing ops. Require reason codes on every rejected MQL.

3. **High form-fill volume from content syndication with near-zero downstream conversion** — Content syndication leads often have 2-5% MQL-to-SQL conversion rates vs. 15-25% for inbound. Teams treating syndication leads identically to inbound demo requests create massive sales capacity waste. These leads require dedicated, longer nurture tracks (12-16 weeks minimum).

4. **Gated content generating "leads" who downloaded a PDF but have zero buying intent** — The 2025 industry shift: top-performing teams are ungating 80%+ of content and reserving gates only for high-intent offers (product demos, assessments, consultations). Gated whitepapers and ebooks increasingly produce data-exchange contacts, not demand signals.

5. **Pipeline "created" in CRM but never progressing past Stage 1** — Inflated pipeline reporting from premature opportunity creation. If >30% of opportunities created by SDRs are stuck in Stage 1 for more than 2x the average stage duration, the SQL criteria are too loose.

6. **Retargeting budget consuming 25%+ of paid media spend with no attribution to pipeline** — Retargeting often shows strong click metrics but unclear pipeline contribution. Without proper view-through attribution and holdout testing, retargeting spend can silently inflate without proving incremental value.

7. **Single-threaded deals where only one contact per account is engaged** — In B2B buying committees of 10-11 stakeholders, engaging only the champion leaves deals vulnerable to internal blockers. Demand generation programs must be designed to reach multiple personas within each target account simultaneously.

8. **Nurture sequences running identical content to every persona** — A CFO evaluator and a technical end-user need fundamentally different content. Flat nurture tracks that ignore persona segmentation generate unsubscribes (2-3x higher) and fail to advance buying group consensus.

9. **Intent data triggering outbound to accounts with no prior brand awareness** — Cold outbound to accounts showing third-party intent signals (researching your category on G2/TrustRadius) without any brand foundation produces very low connect rates (<2%). These accounts need demand creation exposure (targeted display, content sponsorship) before direct outreach.

10. **Attribution model crediting 100% of pipeline to last-touch demo request** — Last-touch attribution systematically undervalues awareness and consideration programs while over-crediting bottom-funnel conversion points. This distorts budget allocation over time, starving the demand creation programs that fill the top of the funnel 6-12 months later.

11. **Marketing and sales using different opportunity definitions** — Marketing counts "opportunity created" at one stage; sales counts it at another. This produces conflicting pipeline reports and erodes trust. Align on a single, CRM-enforced opportunity creation standard (e.g., must have confirmed next step and identified economic buyer).

12. **Scaling paid spend before organic foundations exist** — Companies that invest heavily in paid acquisition without establishing organic content authority, SEO presence, or social proof find that paid costs increase 30-50% year-over-year as they compete for the same finite audience without compounding organic traffic.

13. **Running ABM programs with a target account list exceeding 5,000 accounts** — ABM loses its differentiation advantage when the account list is too broad. True 1:1 ABM requires 50-200 accounts; 1:few ABM works for 200-1,000. Beyond 1,000, the program is functionally broad-based marketing with an ABM label.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Optimizing for cost-per-lead instead of cost-per-opportunity** — The most destructive metric in demand generation. A $15 CPL from content syndication that converts at 1% to opportunity costs $1,500 per opportunity. A $150 CPL from a targeted webinar that converts at 25% costs $600 per opportunity. CPL optimization inverts actual pipeline economics.

2. **Building the lead scoring model without closed-won/closed-lost analysis** — Teams frequently build scoring models based on assumptions about what behaviors indicate buying intent. The only valid inputs are retrospective analysis of what scored contacts actually did: which behaviors preceded closed-won deals, and which preceded closed-lost or stalled deals.

3. **Treating demand generation as a marketing-only function** — Demand generation fails when marketing operates independently from sales development and revenue operations. The MQL-to-SQL handoff requires co-owned definitions, shared SLAs (e.g., sales must follow up on MQLs within 4 hours), and a feedback loop where sales disposition data flows back to marketing for model refinement.

4. **Launching too many channels simultaneously** — New demand generation programs that attempt to run SEO + paid search + LinkedIn ads + content syndication + webinars + email + events simultaneously within the first quarter spread budget and attention too thin. Launch 2-3 channels, reach baseline performance, then expand.

5. **Neglecting the 95% not in market today** — Teams that focus exclusively on demand capture (the 5% actively buying) neglect building the brand preference and mental availability that determines the pre-contact shortlist. When those 95% do enter a buying cycle, they default to brands they already know and trust.

6. **Using BANT as the sole qualification framework** — BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) was designed for transactional sales. In complex B2B deals, budget is often created (not pre-allocated), authority is distributed across a committee, and timeline is fluid. MEDDPICC or CHAMP frameworks better reflect modern B2B buying dynamics.

7. **Measuring marketing influence on pipeline without controlling for self-selection** — Attributing pipeline to a webinar that a prospect attended 2 days before requesting a demo conflates correlation with causation. The prospect was likely already in evaluation mode. Use incrementality testing and holdout groups to measure true marketing lift.

8. **Running lead nurturing on a fixed calendar cadence instead of behavioral triggers** — Time-based nurture (send email every 5 days regardless of behavior) ignores engagement signals. Trigger-based nurture (send next email when previous email is opened, or when prospect revisits pricing page) produces 3-5x higher engagement rates.

## Regulatory & Compliance Requirements

### Email Communications

- **CAN-SPAM Act (15 U.S.C. §7701-7713)**: Applies to all commercial email including B2B. Requires accurate header information, non-deceptive subject lines, identification as advertisement, valid physical postal address, conspicuous opt-out mechanism, and opt-out processing within 10 business days. Penalties up to $51,744 per violation (adjusted for inflation).
- **GDPR Article 6(1)(f) — Legitimate Interest**: B2B cold email to EU data subjects may be lawful under the legitimate interest basis, but requires a documented Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA) demonstrating that the processing is necessary, proportionate, and does not override data subject rights. The EDPB Guidelines 1/2024 provide updated interpretation. B2C requires explicit opt-in consent.
- **CASL (Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation, S.C. 2010, c.23)**: Requires express or implied consent before sending commercial electronic messages. Implied consent exists for 2 years after a business relationship or 6 months after an inquiry. Penalties up to CAD $10 million per violation for organizations.
- **CCPA/CPRA (Cal. Civ. Code §1798.100-199)**: Requires disclosure of data collection practices and provides California residents right to opt out of sale/sharing of personal information. Impacts third-party data usage in demand generation targeting.

### Telephone Communications

- **TCPA (47 U.S.C. §227)**: Restricts automated or prerecorded calls/texts to cell phones without prior express consent. B2B cold calling to business landlines is generally exempt, but cell phone outreach requires prior express consent. Penalties of $500-$1,500 per violation.

### Data & Privacy

- **Third-party intent data**: Ensure data providers maintain compliant consent chains. Purchasing B2B contact data from providers who collected it without proper consent exposes the buyer to GDPR enforcement risk. Conduct vendor due diligence on data sourcing methodology.
- **Cookie consent**: EU ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC) requires informed consent for non-essential cookies, impacting website tracking, retargeting pixel deployment, and attribution measurement.

## Terminology

- **Demand Creation**: Marketing programs designed to generate new awareness and interest among accounts not yet in an active buying cycle; builds brand preference and mental availability for future purchase decisions.
- **Demand Capture**: Marketing programs targeting accounts already showing active buying intent; converts existing demand into pipeline through high-intent offers like demos, trials, and consultations.
- **MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead)**: A contact who meets predefined fit criteria AND has exhibited behavioral engagement above threshold, indicating readiness for sales development follow-up. Definition must be co-owned by marketing and sales.
- **SAL (Sales Accepted Lead)**: An MQL that an SDR/BDR has reviewed, contacted, and confirmed meets initial qualification criteria. The SAL stage creates accountability — it proves sales received and evaluated the lead.
- **SQL (Sales Qualified Lead)**: A lead that has been validated through direct sales conversation as having confirmed need, authority participation, and active evaluation timeline. Represents genuine buying intent, not just engagement.
- **Pipeline Velocity**: Formula: (Number of Qualified Opportunities × Average Deal Value × Win Rate) ÷ Average Sales Cycle Length in Days. Measures the daily dollar value flowing through the pipeline toward closed revenue.
- **Forrester B2B Revenue Waterfall**: The industry-standard framework (evolved from SiriusDecisions Demand Waterfall) that models the progression of buying groups from initial engagement through revenue. Replaces lead-centric models with buying-group-centric stages.
- **ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)**: A firmographic, technographic, and behavioral definition of the account type most likely to become a high-value, long-retention customer. Built from retrospective analysis of best existing customers, not aspiration.
- **Buying Committee**: The group of stakeholders (typically 10-11 in enterprise B2B) who collectively influence a purchase decision, including champion, economic buyer, technical evaluator, end user, and potential blockers.
- **Lead Scoring**: A quantitative model assigning point values to contacts based on three dimensions — demographic/firmographic fit, third-party intent signals, and behavioral engagement — to prioritize sales follow-up.
- **Multi-Touch Attribution**: A measurement model that distributes pipeline/revenue credit across multiple marketing touchpoints. W-shaped attribution (30/30/30/10 across first touch, lead creation, opportunity creation, and remaining touches) is the recommended starting model.
- **Content Syndication**: Distribution of gated content (whitepapers, reports) through third-party publisher networks to generate contact-level leads matching specified targeting criteria. Produces top-of-funnel leads requiring extended nurture.
- **ABM (Account-Based Marketing)**: A strategic approach that concentrates marketing and sales resources on a defined set of target accounts, delivering personalized campaigns designed to resonate with specific buying committees. Three tiers: 1:1 (strategic, <200 accounts), 1:few (clustered, 200-1,000), 1:many (programmatic, 1,000-5,000).
- **Intent Data**: Third-party signals (from providers like Bombora, G2, or 6sense) indicating that specific accounts are actively researching topics related to your product category. Derived from content consumption patterns across publisher co-ops.
- **SLA (Service Level Agreement)**: In demand generation context, the documented agreement between marketing and sales specifying lead follow-up timeframes (e.g., MQL contacted within 4 hours), required disposition actions, and feedback mechanisms.
- **Progressive Profiling**: A form strategy that collects additional data fields across multiple conversions rather than requesting all information on the first form. Reduces initial form friction while building contact data richness over time.
- **Dark Funnel**: The portion of the buyer journey occurring in channels invisible to marketing attribution — peer conversations, Slack communities, dark social shares, private research. Estimated to represent 60-70% of the actual buying process.
- **MEDDPICC**: Sales qualification framework (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Paper Process, Identify Pain, Champion, Competition) used by enterprise sales teams to validate opportunity quality. More rigorous than BANT for complex B2B deals.
- **Dream 100**: The strategic list of 100 media properties, influencers, communities, events, and publications where ICP accounts spend their time and form opinions. Guides channel selection and partnership strategy.
- **Warm Outbound**: Outbound outreach directed at contacts who have shown some prior engagement signal (website visit, content download, event attendance, intent surge) rather than completely cold contacts. Produces 3-5x higher response rates than pure cold outbound.
- **Cost-Per-Opportunity (CPO)**: Total program cost divided by the number of qualified opportunities (not leads) generated. The primary efficiency metric for demand generation program comparison because it normalizes for downstream quality.
- **SQO (Sales Qualified Opportunity)**: An opportunity that has passed through full sales qualification and entered active pipeline with a defined next step, confirmed stakeholder engagement, and validated business case. Increasingly replacing MQL as the primary marketing target metric.

## Quality Checklist

1. **ICP definition validated against closed-won data** — Verify that ICP criteria reflect actual patterns from your top 20% of customers by revenue/LTV, not hypothetical personas.
2. **Lead scoring model back-tested** — Confirm that the highest-scoring leads in the model actually correlate with higher closed-won rates when analyzed against 6-12 months of historical data.
3. **MQL/SAL/SQL definitions documented and co-signed** — Marketing, SDR leadership, and sales leadership have formally agreed on stage definitions, entry criteria, and SLAs — stored in a shared, versioned document.
4. **Nurture sequences segmented by at least two dimensions** — Verify that nurture tracks vary by both buying stage AND persona/role, not just one dimension.
5. **Attribution model configured and validated** — Confirm that multi-touch attribution is active, that UTM parameters are enforced across all channels, and that attribution data reconciles with CRM pipeline data within 5% variance.
6. **Compliance infrastructure verified** — All commercial emails include physical address, unsubscribe mechanism, and accurate sender information per CAN-SPAM. GDPR Legitimate Interest Assessments documented for EU outreach. CASL consent records maintained.
7. **Sales follow-up SLA monitored** — Dashboard shows real-time MQL follow-up speed. MQLs not contacted within the SLA window (4-6 hours recommended) trigger automated escalation.
8. **Funnel conversion rates benchmarked** — Current stage-to-stage conversion rates have been calculated and compared against industry benchmarks. The single biggest drop-off point has been identified and an improvement hypothesis documented.
9. **Program-level pipeline attribution exists** — Every active demand generation program can report pipeline generated, pipeline influenced, and cost-per-opportunity — not just lead volume or engagement metrics.
10. **Rejected MQL feedback loop operational** — Sales dispositioning rejected MQLs with reason codes, and marketing reviews these codes monthly to adjust scoring, targeting, or nurture strategy.
11. **Content mapped to buying stages** — Each content asset is tagged with its target persona, buying stage, and primary call-to-action. Gaps in the content matrix (persona × stage) are documented.
12. **Intent data integrated and actioned** — Third-party intent signals are flowing into the tech stack and triggering specific workflows (account prioritization, targeted ad activation, SDR outreach sequences) rather than sitting unactioned in a dashboard.
13. **Pipeline velocity tracked monthly** — Pipeline velocity is calculated as a monthly trend metric, with the ability to decompose changes into the four component variables (opportunities, deal size, win rate, cycle length).
14. **Budget allocation reviewed quarterly against CPO by channel** — Marketing budget is redistributed based on actual cost-per-opportunity performance, not historical allocation inertia.
15. **Dark funnel accounted for in strategy** — Self-reported attribution ("How did you hear about us?" as free-text on demo request forms) is collected and analyzed to capture demand sources invisible to digital attribution.
