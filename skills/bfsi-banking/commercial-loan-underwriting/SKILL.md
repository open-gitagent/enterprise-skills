# Commercial Loan Underwriting

## Domain Overview

Commercial loan underwriting is the structured evaluation of a borrower's capacity, character, capital, collateral, and conditions (the "5 Cs") to determine whether extending credit aligns with the institution's risk appetite and regulatory expectations. The discipline spans commercial and industrial (C&I) lending, commercial real estate (CRE) financing, construction and land development loans, asset-based facilities, and leveraged lending. Unlike consumer lending, which relies heavily on automated scoring models, commercial underwriting demands narrative-driven credit analysis where the underwriter synthesizes financial statements, industry dynamics, management quality, and deal structure into a defensible recommendation.

Regulatory agencies — the OCC, FDIC, and Federal Reserve — have increasingly scrutinized commercial lending practices, particularly CRE concentrations and leveraged lending. The 2024 Shared National Credit (SNC) review found that non-pass loans (special mention plus classified) rose to 9.1% of total commitments, with leveraged loans comprising 79% of special mention and 84% of classified exposures. The agencies specifically noted "weak structures reflecting layered risks that include some combination of high leverage levels, aggressive repayment assumptions, and other underwriting weaknesses." In October 2024, the OCC issued Bulletin 2024-29 specifically addressing refinance risk in commercial lending, reflecting heightened concern about maturing CRE loans in a higher interest rate environment.

The December 2025 rescission of the 2013 Interagency Guidance on Leveraged Lending by the OCC and FDIC (notably without the Federal Reserve joining) signals a shift toward principles-based supervision. Banks now must demonstrate sound credit risk management through their own internal frameworks rather than relying on prescriptive guidance. This increases the burden on underwriters to document credit rationale thoroughly, maintain consistent standards, and build defensible files that can withstand both internal credit review and regulatory examination. Institutions with CRE concentrations exceeding the 100/300 thresholds established by OCC Bulletin 2006-46 face mandatory enhanced risk management requirements and heightened supervisory scrutiny.

The adoption of CECL (Current Expected Credit Losses) under FASB ASC 326 has fundamentally changed how loan loss provisioning intersects with underwriting. Underwriters must now consider lifetime expected losses at origination, making initial risk rating accuracy and forward-looking economic forecasts integral to both the credit decision and the institution's financial reporting.

## Core Decision Framework

### The Repayment Source Hierarchy

Regulators (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve, NCUA) require underwriters to identify and prioritize repayment sources in a strict hierarchy:

1. **Primary source — Operating cash flow**: The borrower's demonstrated ability to generate sufficient cash from ongoing business operations to service all debt obligations. Sound underwriting centers on this source above all others.
2. **Secondary source — Collateral liquidation**: Proceeds from the orderly disposition of pledged assets. Collateral protects against loss severity but does not transform a weak credit into an acceptable one.
3. **Tertiary source — Guarantor support**: Personal or corporate guarantees that provide recourse beyond the borrowing entity. Guarantors must be independently analyzed through global cash flow and personal financial statement review.

### The Three-Metric Underwriting Test (CRE)

For income-producing commercial real estate, sound underwriting requires passing all three metrics simultaneously — not just one or two:

- **Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**: NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service. Minimum 1.25× for stabilized properties; community banks should target 1.40×–1.50× to withstand a standard recession. A 1.15× DSCR, common in competitive markets, leaves only 14% margin before the loan becomes non-performing.
- **Loan-to-Value (LTV)**: Loan Amount ÷ Appraised Value. Supervisory limits under 12 CFR 34, Subpart D, Appendix A set maximum LTV at 80% for commercial real estate, 85% for multifamily, and 65% for raw land. Aggregate loans exceeding supervisory LTV limits cannot exceed 100% of total capital.
- **Debt Yield**: NOI ÷ Loan Amount. A leverage-neutral metric unaffected by interest rate or amortization assumptions. National and regional banks use a minimum 8%–10% debt yield floor. A 1.25× DSCR at 5% interest corresponds to roughly 8% debt yield.

### C&I Credit Analysis Logic

For C&I loans, the underwriter evaluates:

- **Cash flow adequacy**: Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio (FCCR) ≥ 1.20× using EBITDA less unfinanced CapEx, taxes, and distributions divided by total debt service plus capital lease obligations.
- **Leverage**: Funded Debt / EBITDA. Typical acceptable range is 2.0×–4.0× depending on industry; above 6.0× is regulatory leveraged lending territory.
- **Liquidity**: Current ratio, quick ratio, working capital trends, and access to committed credit facilities.
- **Trend analysis**: Three-year minimum financial statement history showing revenue trajectory, margin stability, and working capital management.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Application Intake and Eligibility Screening
- Collect borrower and guarantor financial statements (3 years tax returns, interim statements, personal financial statements)
- Verify legal entity structure, ownership chain, and related-party relationships
- Confirm loan purpose aligns with institution's lending policy and risk appetite
- Screen against BSA/AML requirements, OFAC sanctions, and insider lending restrictions (Regulation O, 12 CFR 215)

### Phase 2: Financial Spreading and Analysis
- Spread financial statements into standardized format (RMA or internal template)
- Calculate key ratios: DSCR, FCCR, leverage, liquidity, profitability margins
- Perform trend analysis across minimum 3-year period
- Reconcile tax returns to financial statements; investigate material discrepancies
- Prepare global cash flow analysis combining all borrower entities and guarantors into a single consolidated view

### Phase 3: Collateral Evaluation
- Order appraisal compliant with 12 CFR 34, Subpart C (FIRREA requirements) for transactions exceeding $500,000 (real estate secured) or as threshold dictates
- Verify appraiser independence per OCC Bulletin 2010-42 and Interagency Appraisal Guidelines
- Calculate LTV against supervisory limits and internal policy limits
- Assess collateral quality: location, condition, marketability, environmental risk (Phase I/II ESA)
- For non-real-estate collateral: evaluate liquidation value (not going-concern) and apply appropriate advance rates

### Phase 4: Risk Rating Assignment
- Assign initial risk rating using institution's dual risk rating system (borrower risk + facility risk)
- Map to regulatory classification: Pass (with internal sub-grades), Special Mention, Substandard, Doubtful, Loss
- Document rating rationale explicitly; a rating without narrative support fails examiner scrutiny
- Validate rating against OCC Comptroller's Handbook definitions:
  - **Special Mention**: Potential weaknesses deserving close attention; if uncorrected, may result in deterioration
  - **Substandard**: Inadequately protected by current sound worth and paying capacity; well-defined weakness exists
  - **Doubtful**: Full collection highly questionable and improbable based on existing conditions
  - **Loss**: Considered uncollectible; of such little value that continuance as a bankable asset is not warranted

### Phase 5: Credit Memorandum and Structuring
- Draft credit memorandum limited to critical decision elements: borrower overview, risk score, terms, pricing, covenants, key financials, industry context, policy exceptions, and final recommendation
- Structure loan terms: amortization, maturity, rate type, covenants, guaranty requirements, collateral package
- Set financial covenants: minimum DSCR, maximum leverage, minimum net worth, liquidity requirements
- Define reporting covenants: annual audited financials, quarterly interim statements, annual tax returns, rent rolls (CRE), borrowing base certificates (ABL)
- Identify and document any policy exceptions with compensating factors

### Phase 6: Approval and Booking
- Route to appropriate approval authority based on loan size and risk grade
- Obtain credit committee or board approval for loans exceeding delegated authority
- Verify compliance with legal lending limit (12 USC 84 for national banks)
- Ensure proper loan documentation, UCC filings, title insurance, and flood insurance determination (Flood Disaster Protection Act)

## Evaluation Criteria

### Quantitative Scoring Matrix

| Metric | Strong | Acceptable | Marginal | Unacceptable |
|--------|--------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| DSCR (CRE) | ≥1.50× | 1.25×–1.49× | 1.10×–1.24× | <1.10× |
| FCCR (C&I) | ≥1.50× | 1.20×–1.49× | 1.00×–1.19× | <1.00× |
| Leverage (Debt/EBITDA) | <2.0× | 2.0×–3.5× | 3.5×–5.0× | >5.0× |
| LTV (Commercial) | <65% | 65%–75% | 75%–80% | >80% |
| Debt Yield | >12% | 9%–12% | 7%–9% | <7% |
| Current Ratio | >2.0 | 1.5–2.0 | 1.0–1.5 | <1.0 |

### Qualitative Assessment Factors
- Management experience and depth (key-person dependency risk)
- Industry outlook and cyclicality (use NAICS codes for SNC sector classification)
- Customer concentration in revenue (single customer >25% = material risk)
- Geographic market conditions and competitive positioning
- Quality and timeliness of financial reporting
- Litigation, regulatory, and environmental exposure

## Red Flags & Edge Cases

1. **Guarantor cash flow deficit masked by net worth**: A guarantor shows $5M net worth but negative personal cash flow after accounting for all business distributions, personal debt service, and living expenses. The net worth is largely illiquid (real estate equity). Examiners will challenge reliance on this guarantor as a repayment source.

2. **DSCR calculated on pro forma NOI rather than in-place income**: Underwriter uses projected rents from a lease-up property to calculate DSCR of 1.35×, but actual in-place NOI produces a DSCR of 0.85×. Life insurance company and CMBS lenders specifically prohibit pro forma underwriting; bank examiners will require stress-tested, in-place income analysis.

3. **Related-entity debt excluded from global cash flow**: Borrower principal owns three LLCs; the underwriter only analyzes the borrowing entity's cash flow, missing $400K in annual debt service across the other entities. Per OCC examination procedures, global cash flow must incorporate all affiliated entities and personal obligations.

4. **CRE concentration breaching 300% of capital without board-approved risk management framework**: Institution's CRE loans (excluding owner-occupied) reach 320% of total capital with 55% growth over 36 months, triggering both prongs of the OCC Bulletin 2006-46 supervisory criteria. Absence of enhanced concentration risk management (stress testing, market analysis, board reporting) guarantees adverse examination findings.

5. **Interest-only loan structure concealing inadequate cash flow**: A 10-year interest-only CRE loan shows 1.30× DSCR, but converting to a 25-year amortizing basis reveals a 0.95× DSCR. Refinance risk at maturity becomes acute in a rising rate environment (per OCC Bulletin 2024-29 on refinance risk).

6. **Stale appraisal used at renewal**: Bank renews a 5-year CRE loan using the original appraisal despite material changes in market conditions (vacancy spike, comparable sales decline). 12 CFR 34 Subpart C requires a new appraisal when conditions indicate the existing value may be unreliable.

7. **Leveraged loan with covenant-lite structure and incremental debt capacity**: Borrower has 5.5× leverage with no financial maintenance covenants and ability to draw on an accordion facility adding 1.5× additional leverage. The 2024 SNC review flagged this exact structure: layered risks combining high leverage, aggressive repayment assumptions, and weak covenants.

8. **Double-counted income in global cash flow**: K-1 income from a pass-through entity is counted in both the business cash flow and the guarantor's personal tax return analysis, inflating global DSCR by 15%–20%. This is the most common global cash flow error cited by credit reviewers.

9. **Construction loan with no independent cost verification**: Bank funds a $12M construction project based solely on the borrower's cost estimates without an independent third-party cost review or a qualified construction inspector. OCC CRE Handbook specifically requires banks to "assess the feasibility and appropriateness of the project budget."

10. **Declining industry with borrower outperformance narrative**: Underwriter approves a retail brick-and-mortar credit noting the borrower "outperforms peers," but fails to stress-test against sector-wide secular decline (e.g., physical retail contracting 8%–10% annually). No industry adjustment factor applied to projections.

11. **Insider loan missing Regulation O compliance**: Extension of credit to a bank director's LLC fails to obtain prior board approval with the interested party absent from voting. Regulation O violations carry personal liability and civil money penalties under 12 USC 1972.

12. **Environmental liability on collateral**: Phase I ESA identifies a Recognized Environmental Condition (REC) on a gas station property, but underwriter proceeds without Phase II investigation. Lender liability under CERCLA can attach even without ownership.

13. **Loan participations where lead bank's underwriting is accepted without independent analysis**: Participating bank books a $3M participation based solely on the lead bank's credit memo. OCC examiners require participating banks to perform independent credit analysis (OCC Bulletin 2020-50).

## Common Mistakes

- **Anchoring to historical DSCR without stress testing**: Underwriter approves based on a trailing 1.30× DSCR without modeling the impact of a 200bp rate increase on variable-rate or maturing debt. In the current rate environment, a 200bp stress commonly drops DSCR below 1.0×.
- **Failing to recalculate DSCR on a fully-amortizing basis**: Interest-only periods create illusory coverage that vanishes at conversion or maturity.
- **Treating guarantor net worth as a substitute for cash flow**: A guarantor's $10M net worth in illiquid real estate provides no near-term repayment capacity. Examiners evaluate guarantor support through cash flow first, net worth second.
- **Credit memo that summarizes but does not analyze**: Listing financial ratios without explaining what they mean for this specific borrower's risk profile. The credit memo must "tell the story" — converting data into a credit recommendation narrative.
- **Ignoring working capital trends in C&I lending**: A borrower with growing revenue but declining working capital is funding growth through supplier terms or line draws, masking a structural cash flow problem.
- **Policy exception creep without tracking**: Individual exceptions may be justified, but cumulative exceptions across the portfolio signal systematic risk appetite drift. Examiners review exception rates; above 10%–15% of new originations signals loose underwriting discipline.
- **Inadequate sensitivity analysis on cap rates for CRE**: A 100bp cap rate increase on a property valued at a 5.5% cap rate reduces value by approximately 15%, pushing LTV from 75% to 88%.
- **Conflating EBITDA with cash flow**: Adding back non-cash charges without deducting unfinanced capital expenditures, tax payments, and owner distributions overstates available cash by 20%–40% for capital-intensive businesses.

- The 2024 OCC Semiannual Risk Perspective highlights credit risk as a key supervisory focus, specifically "loan underwriting and collateral management practices"

## Terminology

- **DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)**: Net operating income divided by total annual debt service (principal + interest). The single most important quantitative metric in CRE underwriting. Minimum 1.25× is industry standard; 1.0× is breakeven.
- **Global Cash Flow**: Consolidation of all cash inflows and outflows across every entity and individual in a borrowing relationship into a single analysis. Prevents double-counting and reveals hidden cash demands.
- **Debt Yield**: NOI divided by loan amount. A leverage-neutral coverage metric independent of interest rate and amortization; minimum 8%–10% typical for bank CRE.
- **Loan-to-Value (LTV)**: Loan amount divided by appraised value. Supervisory limits set by Interagency Guidelines; exceeding requires tracking against aggregate capital thresholds.
- **Special Mention**: OCC regulatory classification for credits with potential weaknesses deserving management's close attention. Not classified, but a warning grade.
- **Substandard**: Regulatory classification for credits inadequately protected by the borrower's current paying capacity or collateral. A "well-defined weakness" must exist.
- **Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio (FCCR)**: (EBITDA – Unfinanced CapEx – Cash Taxes – Distributions) ÷ (Total Debt Service + Capital Leases). More conservative than DSCR because it captures all cash demands.
- **Concentration Risk**: Aggregate portfolio exposure to a common risk factor (property type, geography, industry, single borrower). OCC Bulletin 2006-46 defines CRE-specific concentration thresholds.
- **Credit Memorandum**: The formal analytical document presenting the underwriter's assessment, risk rating rationale, deal structure, and recommendation. Must "tell the story" of the credit — not merely recite ratios.
- **Shared National Credit (SNC)**: Syndicated loan of $100M+ original commitment shared by three or more regulated institutions. Subject to annual interagency review.
- **Leveraged Lending**: Generally defined as credit to borrowers with total debt/EBITDA exceeding 4.0×–6.0× (institution-defined after 2025 OCC/FDIC guidance rescission). The Federal Reserve still maintains the 2013 definition.
- **Risk Rating Migration**: Movement of a credit between risk grades over time. Transition matrices track upgrade/downgrade velocity and inform CECL modeling.
- **Covenant Lite (Cov-Lite)**: Loan structures lacking financial maintenance covenants (borrower only tested upon an incurrence event, not periodically). Flagged repeatedly in SNC reviews as a structural weakness.
- **Debt Service Reserve**: Cash or letter of credit set aside to cover a specified number of months of debt service, typically 6–12 months. Required for construction loans, lease-up properties, and weaker credits.
- **Owner-Occupied CRE**: Commercial real estate where >50% of the space is occupied by the borrower's own business operations. Excluded from CRE concentration calculations under OCC Bulletin 2006-46.
- **Advance Rate**: Percentage of collateral value a lender will lend against in asset-based lending. Typical: 80%–85% on eligible A/R, 50%–65% on eligible inventory, 75%–80% on equipment at orderly liquidation value.
- **Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)**: NOI ÷ Property Value. Used to derive property value in CRE appraisals via the income approach. A 100bp increase in cap rate on a 6% cap property reduces value by approximately 14%.
- **Stress Testing (Loan-Level)**: Modeling the impact of adverse scenarios (rate increases, NOI declines, cap rate expansion, vacancy increases) on individual loan metrics to assess vulnerability.
- **Participation**: An undivided interest in a loan originated by another institution. OCC requires purchasing institutions to conduct independent credit analysis, not rely solely on the lead bank.
- **UCA (Uniform Credit Analysis) Cash Flow**: A standardized cash flow methodology that starts with net income and adjusts for non-cash items, working capital changes, CapEx, and debt service to determine a borrower's true repayment capacity. Preferred over simple EBITDA for C&I credits.
- **Policy Exception**: Any approved deviation from the institution's written lending policy (LTV limits, DSCR floors, documentation requirements). Must be individually approved, documented with compensating factors, and tracked in aggregate.
- **Nonaccrual**: Regulatory status applied when full collection of principal or interest is doubtful, or when payment is 90+ days past due. Interest income recognition ceases; reversal of previously accrued interest may be required.

## Quality Checklist

1. ☐ Global cash flow analysis completed — all borrower entities and guarantors consolidated into a single cash flow; K-1 income not double-counted
2. ☐ DSCR calculated on both actual in-place income AND stressed basis (200bp rate increase, 10% NOI decline, or applicable stress per policy)
3. ☐ LTV verified against supervisory limits in Appendix A to 12 CFR 34 Subpart D; any exceptions documented with compensating factors and counted toward aggregate limits
4. ☐ Risk rating supported by written narrative tying financial analysis to the specific rating definition — not just a ratio-based assignment
5. ☐ Credit memorandum includes industry analysis with forward-looking assessment, not boilerplate language recycled from prior memos
6. ☐ Appraisal or evaluation meets FIRREA requirements; appraiser independence verified; appraisal age appropriate for current market conditions
7. ☐ CRE concentration impact assessed — loan's contribution to institution's 100/300 thresholds calculated and documented
8. ☐ Flood determination obtained; if property in SFHA, evidence of adequate flood insurance documented prior to closing
9. ☐ Regulation O screening completed for insider relationships; prior board approval obtained where required
10. ☐ Covenant package includes measurable financial triggers (minimum DSCR, maximum leverage, net worth floors) with clearly defined testing periods and remedy provisions
11. ☐ Legal lending limit calculation documented showing total exposure (direct + indirect + contingent) to the borrower and related interests remains within 12 USC 84 limits
12. ☐ For participations: independent credit analysis performed by purchasing institution — not reliance on lead bank's memorandum alone
13. ☐ Environmental due diligence (Phase I ESA minimum) completed for all real estate collateral; RECs identified and addressed
14. ☐ Tax return-to-financial statement reconciliation completed; material discrepancies investigated and resolved
15. ☐ CECL implications assessed — initial risk rating and expected loss estimate documented for allowance methodology input
